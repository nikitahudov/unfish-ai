## Introduction

In a world of YouTube videos and Twitch streams, poker books might seem outdated. They're not.

The best poker books contain deep, structured knowledge that takes years to accumulate. They provide the theoretical foundation that videos build upon.

<KeyConcept title="Why Books Still Matter">
**Books offer what videos can't:**

- **Depth:** Authors can develop ideas fully, not in 10-minute chunks
- **Structure:** Organized progression from concept to concept
- **Reference:** Return to specific sections when needed
- **Permanence:** Great books remain relevant for decades
- **Your pace:** Study when and how you want

The top players read. The books in this module have shaped how poker is played today.
</KeyConcept>

This module teaches you which books to read, how to read them effectively, and how to extract maximum value.

## The Essential Reading List

<KeyConcept title="Recommended Books by Level">
**Beginner (foundational concepts):**
- "The Theory of Poker" - David Sklansky (fundamental theorem, key concepts)
- "Harrington on Hold'em Vol 1-3" - Dan Harrington (tournament, but concepts apply)
- "Essential Poker Math" - Alton Hardin (accessible math for new players)

**Intermediate (deeper strategy):**
- "Applications of No-Limit Hold'em" - Matthew Janda (range-based thinking)
- "The Mental Game of Poker 1 & 2" - Jared Tendler (psychology)
- "Modern Poker Theory" - Michael Acevedo (GTO foundations)

**Advanced (mastery level):**
- "Play Optimal Poker" - Andrew Brokos (practical GTO)
- "No-Limit Hold'em for Advanced Players" - Sklansky & Miller
- "Expert Heads Up No Limit Hold'em" - Will Tipton (specialized)
</KeyConcept>

### Why "The Theory of Poker" First?

Sklansky's classic introduces the **Fundamental Theorem of Poker:**

*"Every time you play a hand differently from the way you would have played it if you could see all your opponents' cards, they gain; and every time you play your hand the same way you would have played it if you could see all their cards, they lose."*

This single idea underlies all poker strategy. Start here.

### Why Mental Game Matters

Most players focus 90% on strategy, 10% on mental game.

Optimal might be 60/40 or even 50/50.

You can know all the right plays but execute poorly when tilted, scared, or overconfident. Mental game books are not optional—they're essential.

## How to Read Poker Books

<KeyConcept title="Active Reading Methods">
**Passive reading = forgetting everything.**

Active reading techniques:

1. **Preview:** Skim chapter headings before reading
2. **Question:** Ask "What will I learn here?"
3. **Highlight:** Mark key concepts (sparingly!)
4. **Annotate:** Write notes in margins
5. **Summarize:** Write chapter summary in your words
6. **Apply:** Ask "How does this change my game?"
</KeyConcept>

### The SQ3R Method

A proven study technique adapted for poker:

**S - Survey:** Quick skim of the chapter
- Read headings, subheadings
- Look at diagrams/examples
- Get the big picture first

**Q - Question:** What do I want to learn?
- Turn headings into questions
- "C-Betting Strategy" → "When should I c-bet?"

**R - Read:** Active, focused reading
- One section at a time
- Pause to think about concepts

**R - Recite:** Explain in your own words
- Close the book
- Summarize what you just read
- Write key points without looking

**R - Review:** Revisit later
- Review notes before next chapter
- Re-read difficult sections
- Apply concepts at the table

<Exercise

### Highlighting Done Right

**Bad highlighting:** Marking half the book yellow
**Good highlighting:** 1-2 key points per page maximum

Your highlights should:
- Capture core concepts, not details
- Be things you want to remember
- Enable quick review later
- Not require re-reading surrounding text

### Outdated vs Timeless

**Timeless (always relevant):**
- Fundamental concepts (pot odds, position)
- Mental game principles
- Mathematical foundations
- Decision-making frameworks

**Potentially outdated:**
- Specific hand ranges
- Bet sizing recommendations
- Pre-solver strategy
- Online tells and timing

**Rule of thumb:** Books focused on CONCEPTS age well. Books focused on SPECIFIC PLAYS may need updating.

- **MTT Community** for tournament strategy
- **Be specific when posting hands:** Include reads, stats, stakes

## Extracting Maximum Value

### Don't Read Passively

After each chapter, ask:

1. **What were the 3 main points?**
2. **What do I disagree with or question?**
3. **What will I do differently?**
4. **What do I need to study more?**

### Create Action Items

**Bad approach:** Read chapter, think "that was interesting," move on.

**Good approach:** Read chapter, create specific action items.

**Example action items after reading about c-betting:**
- "Review my c-bet frequency in HM3 - is it over 70%?"
- "Practice checking back more on wet boards"
- "Study c-bet sizing based on board texture"

<Exercise

### The Re-Read Strategy

Great books reveal more each time you read them.

**First read:** Get the main concepts
**Second read (after improving):** See things you missed
**Third read:** Deep nuances and advanced applications

Schedule annual re-reads of your top 3-5 books.

## Building a Reading Habit

### Making It Stick

**1. Schedule dedicated time**
- 20-30 minutes daily
- Same time each day (morning coffee, before bed)
- Treat it as non-negotiable

**2. Create a reading environment**
- Quiet, no distractions
- No phone nearby
- Good lighting, comfortable seat

**3. Track progress**
- Keep a reading log
- Note completion dates
- Write brief reviews

**4. Join a community**
- Poker book clubs exist
- Find a study partner
- Discuss what you're reading

### Suggested Reading Schedule

**Month 1-2:**
- "The Theory of Poker" - Sklansky (2-3 weeks)
- "Essential Poker Math" - Hardin (1-2 weeks)

**Month 3-4:**
- "Harrington on Hold'em Vol 1" (2-3 weeks)
- "The Mental Game of Poker" - Tendler (3-4 weeks)

**Month 5-6:**
- "Applications of No-Limit Hold'em" - Janda (4-6 weeks - dense!)

**Ongoing:**
- Articles and forums supplement books
- Re-read fundamentals annually

## Summary

Books provide the deep, structured foundation that other study methods build upon. Reading effectively requires active engagement, not passive consumption.

**Key takeaways:**
- Start with fundamentals (Theory of Poker, Essential Math)
- Mental game books are essential, not optional
- Read actively: preview, question, highlight, summarize, apply
- Great books reward multiple readings
- Create action items from every chapter
- Build a consistent reading habit (20-30 min daily)
- Supplement books with quality articles

**Your reading assignment:**
- Pick one book from the beginner list
- Read the first chapter using the SQ3R method
- Write a summary and 3 action items
- Start a reading schedule you can maintain

The players who study consistently become the players who win consistently.
