## Introduction

Poker isn't played against cards—it's played against **people**.

The same hand can be a clear fold against one opponent and a clear value bet against another. The difference? Knowing who you're playing against.

<KeyConcept title="Why Player Profiling Matters">
**The core truth:** Your strategy should change based on your opponent.

- Against a player who never folds: value bet relentlessly, never bluff
- Against a player who folds too much: bluff frequently, don't overvalue hands
- Against a balanced player: play solid, don't try fancy exploits

**Accurate profiling = exploitative edge.** The better you know your opponents, the more money you make.
</KeyConcept>

In Module 5.2, you learned to extract information from showdowns. Now we'll turn that information into actionable player profiles that guide every decision.

## The Player Type Framework

Most players fall into recognizable categories. Knowing these archetypes lets you quickly adjust your strategy.

<KeyConcept title="The Six Player Types">
**1. TAG (Tight-Aggressive)**
- VPIP: 15-22%, PFR: 13-20%
- Plays few hands but plays them aggressively
- Solid, winning style at most stakes
- Hard to exploit, respect their raises

**2. LAG (Loose-Aggressive)**
- VPIP: 25-35%, PFR: 20-30%
- Plays many hands, aggressive with wide range
- Unpredictable and dangerous
- Can be very good or very bad depending on skill

**3. Calling Station**
- VPIP: 30-50%, PFR: 5-15% (large VPIP-PFR gap)
- Calls too much, rarely raises, never folds
- Don't bluff them—value bet relentlessly
- Your ATM machine

**4. Nit**
- VPIP: <12%, PFR: 8-12%
- Plays only premium hands
- When they bet big, they have it
- Steal their blinds, fold to their aggression

**5. Maniac**
- VPIP: 40%+, PFR: 30%+
- Raises constantly with any two cards
- Highly aggressive, often reckless
- Tighten up, let them hang themselves

**6. Weak-Tight (Rock)**
- VPIP: 15-20%, PFR: 8-14% (passive)
- Plays tight but scared
- Folds to aggression, rarely bluffs
- Easy to push around
</KeyConcept>

## Quick Profiling Methods

You don't need 1,000 hands to profile someone. Use these quick methods:

### First Orbit Observations

In your first 10-15 hands at a table, notice:
- How many pots does each player enter?
- Are they raising or limping?
- Any showdowns yet? What did they show?
- Who's chatting? (Often recreational players)
- Stack sizes? (Short stacks often scared/recreational)

### The VPIP-PFR Estimation

Without software, estimate these stats:

**VPIP (Voluntarily Put $ In Pot):**
- Count how many hands they play in one orbit (9-10 hands)
- 2 out of 10 = ~20% VPIP
- 4 out of 10 = ~40% VPIP

**PFR (Pre-Flop Raise):**
- Count how often they raise when they enter
- If they enter 4 times and raise 3 times = ~75% aggression (LAG/TAG)
- If they enter 4 times and raise 1 time = ~25% aggression (passive)

### vs Maniac

**Key adjustments:**
- Tighten starting hand requirements
- Call down lighter (they bluff too much)
- Let them build the pot, then trap
- Don't try to out-aggress them
- Be prepared for high variance

### vs Weak-Tight

**Key adjustments:**
- C-bet almost every flop (they fold)
- Steal their blinds relentlessly
- Apply pressure in every pot
- Rarely need to show down
- Don't give them free cards

## Key Statistics Reference

Here's a quick reference for interpreting player stats:

### VPIP Ranges

| VPIP | Classification | Player Type |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| <12% | Very Tight | Nit |
| 12-18% | Tight | TAG or Weak-Tight |
| 19-25% | Normal | TAG or competent LAG |
| 26-35% | Loose | LAG or Station |
| >35% | Very Loose | Maniac or Fish |

### PFR and Gap

| VPIP-PFR Gap | Meaning |
|--------------|---------|
| 0-4% | Very aggressive (raises almost always) |
| 5-8% | Normal aggression |
| 9-15% | Somewhat passive |
| >15% | Very passive (calling station likely) |

### Quick Profile Formula

**VPIP under 15% + folds to 3-bets = Nit**
**VPIP 15-25% + small gap = TAG**
**VPIP 25%+ + small gap = LAG**
**VPIP 25%+ + large gap = Calling Station**
**VPIP 40%+ + constantly raising = Maniac**

## Building Profiles Over Time

Initial reads are tentative. Confidence builds with more data.

<KeyConcept title="Information Hierarchy">
**Most reliable → Least reliable:**

1. **Showdown hands** — What they actually had
2. **Betting actions** — How they played it
3. **Timing tells** — How long they took
4. **Chat behavior** — What they said
5. **Appearance** — Age, clothing (live only, unreliable)

Weight showdowns heavily. One showdown is worth 20 hands of observation without showdown.
</KeyConcept>

### Confidence Levels

| Hands Observed | Confidence |
|----------------|------------|
| 5-15 hands | Tentative guess |
| 15-30 hands | Reasonable read |
| 30-50 hands | Confident profile |
| 50+ hands | Strong profile |
| 100+ hands | Very reliable |

**Note:** One showdown can change everything. A player you thought was tight showing down 74s for a bluff should dramatically update your read.

## Updating Profiles

Players aren't static. Update your reads based on:

### Dynamic Factors

**Tilt:** A TAG on tilt becomes a LAG or Maniac
**Winning/Losing:** Winners loosen up, losers tighten or tilt
**Time:** Late-night players often looser/tired
**Alcohol:** Live tells—drinking affects play
**Boredom:** Long sessions → looser play

### When to Update

- After every showdown
- When behavior contradicts profile
- After significant wins/losses
- When they change seats or return from break
- When game dynamics shift

### Avoiding Profile Lock-In

**Common mistake:** "I labeled them a nit, so I'll always treat them as a nit."

**Better approach:** "My current read is nit, but I'll update if I see contradictory evidence."

Stay flexible. The best read is the current one, not the first one.

## Common Profiling Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Over-Generalizing from Small Samples

Seeing one bluff doesn't make them a maniac. Seeing one fold doesn't make them a nit. Need 30+ hands for confident reads.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Contradictory Evidence

If your "nit" shows down a bluff, don't ignore it because it doesn't fit your narrative. Update immediately.

### Mistake 3: Stereotyping

Age, gender, appearance, nationality—none of these reliably predict playing style. A 60-year-old woman can be a LAG; a young guy in a hoodie can be a nit.

### Mistake 4: Confirmation Bias

Don't remember the hands that confirm your read while forgetting the ones that contradict it. All data matters.

### Mistake 5: Online ≠ Live Profiles

Someone's online style may differ from their live style. Different environments, different plays. Profile them fresh in each context.

## Summary

<KeyConcept title="Key Takeaways">
1. **Know the six types:** TAG, LAG, Calling Station, Nit, Maniac, Weak-Tight
2. **Profile quickly:** Use VPIP/PFR estimation from first orbits
3. **Adjust specifically:** Different types require opposite strategies
4. **Value showdowns:** One showdown > 20 hands of observation
5. **Stay flexible:** Update reads as new information arrives
6. **Avoid stereotypes:** Profile based on actions, not appearance
7. **Watch for dynamics:** Tilt, winning/losing, fatigue change profiles
</KeyConcept>

Player profiling is the bridge between poker theory and real-world profit. The same strategy that crushes a calling station will get destroyed by a LAG. Know your opponent, adjust your strategy, and extract maximum value.
